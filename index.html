    Claim IV in Depth · Multiplication of Species

    How new species form

    Darwin realized species diverge from a common ancestor into new, distinct species. Today we see this through two pathways — allopatric (geographic isolation) and sympatric (within the same area).

    ⛰

    Allopatric Speciation

    Physical barriers like rivers, mountains, or human-caused fragmentation separate populations. Isolated groups face different selective pressures, causing distinct species to arise over time.

    Real World Example

    Polar Bears & Grizzly Bears

    They share a common ancestor but became two species. During the ice ages, grizzlies were geographically isolated in an arctic environment through Pleistocene glaciations (Fonseca et al., 2023). A growing appetite for seals also contributed (PBS WHYY, 2008).

    🍎

    Sympatric Speciation

    New species emerge without physical barriers, often when parts of a population specialize in different resources or food within the same area.

    Real World Example

    The Apple Maggot Fly

    The North American apple maggot fly diverged within the same area. Originally feeding on hawthorn fruit, some shifted to apple trees introduced in the 19th century. Because young flies favor the scent of the fruit they hatched on, apple-born and hawthorn-born flies split apart (Berkeley UC, n.d.).

    Domain Classification Model

    How life is organized today

    Carl Linnaeus proposed a classification system in 1753. Two and a half centuries later, genetic technology has rewritten it from physical traits alone to a system grounded in DNA.

    The Linnaean Hierarchy — from broadest to most specific

    Taxonomy is the science of naming, describing, and classifying organisms. Modern tools — DNA and RNA sequencing — group species by shared genes, not just shared appearance.
